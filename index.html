Essay for Chapter 3
In Chapter 3, Cairo talks about the truth continuum. First, he says that "Any visualization is a model" (P104) by giving an example of a locator map. A model should be needlessly complex for its audience to better and easier understand. Then, he analyzes that it is not enough to create a satisfactory model using a single dataset. For example, in "Percent change in traffic fatalities 1975-2012", the number of vehicles is different in different cities. It is necessary to control variables like this, generating a relative instead. It is more accurate to reveal the truth. Cairo introduces three reasons why people are often mistaken. They are the patternicity bug, the storytelling bug, and the confirmation bug. Patternicity means that people always try to find something interesting. They believe the pattern exists even if there is no pattern at all. The second one is storytelling, which indicates that people are willing to believe in what they want to believe. Cairo says that "...it is dangerous if it blinds us toward evidence that should compel us to tweak or discard our models" (P123). The last one is confirmation. People refuse to give their explanation up even something conflicts with their explanation. Finally, Cairo raises that "Truth is neither absolute, nor relative" (P126). Because the model is between "absolutely untrue" and "absolutely true", we need to collect more information to make the model move closer to "absolutely true". Cairo suggests that we should stop and think after we find something interesting, and "explore your information at multiple levels of depth and breadth" (P136).
Sometimes, oversimplifying data help us tell the truth better because the audience doesn't want to see something complex. For example, the map that displayed the results of the 2012 parliamentary elections. It is easy to get the result and understand the author's purpose directly.
